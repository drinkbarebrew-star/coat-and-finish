How Much Does Interior Painting Cost in Chicago? (2026 Price Guide)

June 25, 2026 • 8 min read • Interior Painting

If you are a Chicago homeowner planning to refresh your interior, the first question on your mind is almost certainly about cost. Interior painting prices can vary widely depending on the size of your home, the condition of your walls, and the quality of materials you choose. In a city like Chicago, where older homes with plaster walls sit next to modern condos with drywall, the range is even broader than the national average.

This guide breaks down everything you need to know about interior painting costs in Chicago for 2026, from room-by-room pricing to the factors that push estimates higher or lower. Whether you are painting a single bedroom or refreshing an entire home, you will walk away with a clear understanding of what to budget.

Average Interior Painting Costs in Chicago (2026)

As of 2026, Chicago homeowners can expect to pay between $2 and $6 per square foot for professional interior painting. This range accounts for standard walls with average prep work. For a single room, most projects land between $300 and $800. A full-home interior repaint for a typical Chicago bungalow or two-flat ranges from $3,000 to $8,000, depending on square footage and complexity.

These prices include labor, paint, and basic preparation such as taping, filling small nail holes, and protecting furniture and floors. More extensive prep work, like repairing plaster cracks or sanding rough surfaces, will increase the total cost. Premium paint brands like Benjamin Moore Regal Select or Sherwin-Williams Emerald will also push costs toward the higher end of the range, though they typically deliver better coverage and longer-lasting results.

Room-by-Room Cost Breakdown

Not every room costs the same to paint. Kitchens and bathrooms require more preparation because of moisture and grease buildup, while bedrooms and hallways are generally more straightforward. Here is a breakdown of what to expect for each room in a typical Chicago home:

Room Average Cost Notes
Bedroom $350 – $700 Standard size, 2 coats, walls only
Living Room $500 – $900 Larger walls, may include accent wall
Kitchen $400 – $800 Extra prep for grease and moisture
Bathroom $300 – $600 Smaller space, moisture-resistant paint
Hallway $250 – $500 Narrow space, may include stairwell
Dining Room $400 – $750 Medium size, may include chair rail
Whole Home (3 BR) $3,000 – $8,000 All rooms, hallways, closets, trim

These figures assume standard 8-foot ceilings. Many older Chicago homes, particularly Victorians, greystones, and pre-war apartments, have 9- or 10-foot ceilings that increase both the paintable area and the labor required.

Factors That Affect Interior Painting Cost

Every painting project is different, and several variables can move your estimate significantly in either direction. Here are the most important factors:

1. Room Size and Ceiling Height

This is the most obvious factor. A larger room has more wall surface to cover, which means more paint and more labor hours. Rooms with ceilings above 8 feet require ladders or scaffolding, which slows the crew down and increases the price. Many Chicago greystones and vintage apartments have ceilings reaching 10 or even 12 feet, which can add 20 to 30 percent to the per-room cost.

2. Wall Condition and Prep Work

If your walls have cracks, water stains, peeling paint, or uneven surfaces, the crew will need to spend time patching, sanding, and priming before they can start painting. In older Chicago homes with original plaster walls, prep work can account for 30 to 50 percent of the total project time. Homes with smooth drywall in good condition require far less preparation.

3. Paint Quality

There is a real difference between builder-grade paint and premium formulations. A gallon of basic latex paint might cost $25 to $35, while a gallon of Benjamin Moore Regal Select or Sherwin-Williams Duration runs $55 to $80. Premium paints cover better, last longer, and are easier to clean. For most homeowners, the upfront cost difference is worth the improved durability and finish.

4. Number of Colors

Using one color throughout the home is the most cost-effective approach. Each additional color requires separate mixing, additional taping, and careful cut-in work at color transitions. An accent wall or two will not dramatically increase the price, but painting every room a different color can add 10 to 20 percent to the total estimate.

5. Trim, Doors, and Windows

Many homeowners focus on wall painting costs and forget about trim. Painting baseboards, crown molding, window frames, and interior doors adds labor and materials. Expect to add $1 to $3 per linear foot for trim and $75 to $150 per door. If your home has detailed millwork or built-in shelving, the cost will be higher due to the careful brushwork required.

6. Wallpaper Removal

If your walls currently have wallpaper, removing it before painting adds both time and cost to the project. Wallpaper removal in Chicago typically runs $1 to $3 per square foot, depending on how many layers are present and whether the wallpaper was properly installed over primer.

DIY vs. Professional Painting: Is It Worth Hiring a Pro?

Many homeowners consider handling interior painting themselves to save money. And for a single, small room with walls in good condition, DIY can be a reasonable choice. But before you head to the hardware store, consider these factors:

Time commitment: A professional crew can paint a typical room in 4 to 6 hours. Most DIY painters spend an entire weekend on the same room, accounting for taping, moving furniture, painting, and cleanup. A whole-home project that takes a professional team 3 to 5 days can easily stretch to 3 to 4 weekends for a DIYer.

Quality of results: Professional painters deliver clean, even coverage with sharp lines and no drips. They know how to cut in around trim without tape, how to avoid roller marks, and how to handle tricky areas like stairwells and high ceilings. The difference between a professional job and an amateur one is visible to anyone who looks closely.

Hidden costs of DIY: When you add up paint, rollers, brushes, tape, drop cloths, primer, sandpaper, and a ladder, materials for a DIY project often run $150 to $300 per room. Factor in the value of your time and the risk of needing to redo sections, and the savings over hiring a professional narrow considerably.

Prep work expertise: The most critical part of any paint job is the preparation, and this is where professional painters truly earn their fee. Properly patching drywall, repairing plaster, and priming stains requires experience and the right materials. Skipping or rushing prep work leads to results that look good initially but fail within a year or two.

Tips to Get the Best Value on Interior Painting

Whether you are working with a tight budget or simply want to maximize your investment, these strategies can help you get the most for your money:

  • Get at least three estimates. Prices vary between contractors, and comparing quotes helps you understand the market rate for your specific project. Be wary of estimates that are dramatically lower than the rest, as they often indicate shortcuts in materials or preparation.
  • Bundle rooms together. Painting multiple rooms at once is more cost-effective per room than painting them individually. Contractors can work more efficiently when they have a larger scope, and you save on setup and cleanup time.
  • Choose your paint wisely. Mid-range paints from trusted brands like Benjamin Moore or Sherwin-Williams offer the best balance of quality and value. You do not need the most expensive line for every room, but avoid the cheapest options, as they require more coats and wear faster.
  • Handle the easy prep yourself. Moving furniture away from walls, removing outlet covers, and taking down curtains and wall art before the crew arrives can save an hour or more of billable labor.
  • Schedule during the off-season. Many Chicago painting companies are busiest from May through September. Scheduling your interior project during the fall or winter months can sometimes yield lower pricing or faster availability.
  • Skip the accent walls sparingly. While an accent wall adds visual interest, each additional color increases labor time. If budget is a concern, stick to one or two colors for the entire project.
