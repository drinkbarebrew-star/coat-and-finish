Should You Paint Before Selling Your Home? ROI Breakdown

The data-backed case for fresh paint when preparing your Chicago home for the market.

Home Selling • May 20, 2026

Preparing Your Home for Sale: Where Paint Fits In

When it comes time to sell your home, the list of potential improvements can feel endless. New countertops, updated fixtures, landscaping, staging, deep cleaning, and repairs all compete for your limited pre-sale budget. Among all these options, fresh paint consistently ranks as one of the highest-return investments a seller can make.

Real estate agents know this instinctively. Walk through any list of pre-sale recommendations from a top Chicago agent and painting will appear near the top, often as the number one suggestion. But what does the data actually say? Is the return on investment as strong as agents claim, and which rooms should you prioritize if your budget does not allow for a full repaint?

This guide breaks down the numbers, identifies the rooms and colors that matter most for resale, and helps you decide whether a professional paint job is the right investment before listing your Chicago home.

The ROI of Painting Before Selling

According to data from the National Association of Realtors, interior painting delivers an average return on investment of 107 percent. That means for every dollar you spend on interior paint, you can expect to recover that dollar plus an additional seven cents in your sale price. Few home improvement projects can match that figure.

Exterior painting performs even better in some markets, with reported returns ranging from 100 to 150 percent depending on the condition of the existing paint and the local real estate market. In a competitive market like Chicago, where buyers often compare dozens of listings online before scheduling a single showing, the visual impact of fresh paint in listing photos can be the difference between a click and a scroll-past.

Beyond the direct return on the investment, painting delivers several indirect benefits that are harder to quantify but equally important. Freshly painted homes photograph better, which leads to more online engagement and more showing requests. They feel cleaner and better maintained during walkthroughs, which builds buyer confidence. And they remove one negotiation lever that buyers frequently use to request price reductions. Scuffed, chipped, or dated paint gives buyers an easy excuse to submit a lower offer.

Interior vs. Exterior: Which Matters More for Resale?

If you can only choose one, interior painting typically delivers more impact per dollar. Buyers spend more time evaluating interiors, both online and in person, and the interior is where they will imagine themselves living. Dated wall colors, visible scuffs, worn trim, and patchy touch-ups all register as deferred maintenance in a buyer's mind, even if the underlying structure is perfectly sound.

That said, exterior paint matters significantly for curb appeal. If your exterior paint is peeling, fading, or visibly weathered, it creates a negative first impression before buyers even walk through the door. In Chicago, where harsh winters and freeze-thaw cycles take a toll on exterior surfaces, many homes show visible wear after five to seven years. If your exterior has reached that point, painting it before listing is a strong investment.

The ideal scenario, when budget allows, is to paint both interior and exterior. But if you must choose, focus on the interior first and address any glaring exterior issues second.

Best Paint Colors for Resale in 2026

The color you choose matters almost as much as the decision to paint. When selling a home, the goal is to appeal to the widest possible audience, which means setting aside personal preferences in favor of broadly appealing neutrals.

Warm whites are the dominant choice for resale in 2026. Colors like Benjamin Moore White Dove, Sherwin-Williams Alabaster, and Behr Blank Canvas create a bright, clean backdrop that feels modern without being cold. These warm whites work across architectural styles, from vintage Chicago bungalows to modern high-rise condos.

Light greige, a blend of gray and beige, remains a strong option for sellers who want something slightly warmer and more grounded than pure white. Sherwin-Williams Accessible Beige and Benjamin Moore Revere Pewter are perennial favorites that photograph well and complement a wide range of furniture styles.

For trim, doors, and molding, crisp white in a semi-gloss finish remains the standard. It provides clean contrast against wall colors and signals attention to detail. If the home has higher-end finishes, painting interior doors in a deep tone like black or dark charcoal can add a premium touch that resonates with design-savvy buyers.

Rooms That Matter Most

Not every room carries equal weight in a buyer's evaluation. If you are prioritizing your painting budget, focus on these spaces first.

The kitchen is the most scrutinized room in any home sale. Fresh paint on walls and especially on cabinets can modernize a dated kitchen for a fraction of the cost of a full renovation. White or light gray painted cabinets with modern hardware are one of the highest-impact, lowest-cost upgrades available to sellers. Bathrooms come in a close second. Buyers pay close attention to bathrooms, and scuffed, peeling, or discolored paint immediately signals neglect. Fresh paint in bathrooms reads as clean and well-maintained.

The entry and front hallway set the tone for the entire showing experience. First impressions form within seconds of walking through the door, and a freshly painted entryway signals pride of ownership from the very first step. The living room and primary bedroom round out the priority list. These are the rooms buyers spend the most time visualizing themselves in, and neutral, fresh paint makes that visualization easier.

What NOT to Do When Painting to Sell

Several common mistakes can undermine the value of a pre-sale paint job. Choosing bold or personalized colors is the most frequent error. That accent wall in deep burgundy or the neon nursery may reflect your taste, but it limits the buyer pool. Every buyer who walks in and thinks "I would have to repaint that" is mentally adding cost to your home, which works against you in negotiations.

Using cheap paint is another misstep. Bargain paints show brush strokes, provide uneven coverage, and often require more coats to achieve a finished look. The savings on materials are more than offset by the inferior result. Professional-grade paints from Benjamin Moore or Sherwin-Williams provide better coverage, richer color, and a more durable finish that holds up through the showing process.

Attempting a DIY paint job when you lack experience can also backfire. Buyers and their agents can spot amateur work, including uneven edges, paint on trim, drips, and missed spots. A sloppy paint job suggests that other maintenance has been equally casual, which erodes buyer confidence. If painting is not your strong suit, hiring a professional crew is the better investment.

Finally, do not skip surface preparation. Painting over holes, cracks, peeling paint, or dirty walls produces a result that looks worse than the original condition. Proper preparation, including patching, sanding, cleaning, and priming, is essential for a finish that looks professional and lasts through the selling process.

Professional Painting vs. DIY When Selling

The choice between professional and DIY painting depends on the scope of the project, your skill level, and your timeline. For a single room touch-up with minor scuffs, a capable homeowner can produce acceptable results. For a whole-home repaint or any work that involves cabinets, high ceilings, or detailed trim, professional painters deliver a meaningfully better result.

Professional crews work faster, which matters when you are on a listing timeline. A team of three to four painters can repaint an entire home interior in three to five days. A solo DIY effort on the same home might stretch across two to three weekends, delaying your listing and potentially missing peak market timing.

Professional painters also bring the equipment and expertise to handle difficult areas. Stairwells, vaulted ceilings, detailed crown molding, and multi-story exteriors all require specialized tools and experience that most homeowners do not have.

Timeline: How Far in Advance to Paint

If you have decided to paint before listing, plan the timing carefully. Most professional painting projects for a standard Chicago home can be scheduled and completed within two to three weeks from initial contact. For a whole-home interior, expect three to five days of actual painting work once the crew begins.

Ideally, schedule your painting to finish at least one week before your listing photographer arrives. This allows time for paint to fully cure, for any touch-ups to be completed, and for the new-paint smell to dissipate. You want the home to feel fresh and clean, not like a construction zone.

If you are also staging the home, coordinate with your stager to choose paint colors that complement the staging furniture and decor. Many professional stagers have specific color preferences and will appreciate being consulted before the painting begins.
